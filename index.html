🏛 Choose Your Midpoint Stop
Córdoba was the capital of Moorish Spain from 756 to 1031 — the most sophisticated city in Europe at its peak, with a population of 500,000 when London had 50,000. Two hours is enough to feel it. Park near the Mezquita and walk.
🏛 What to See
Mezquita-Catedral de Córdoba🚶 2 min from Bar Santos — The Great Mosque-Cathedral (begun 785 AD by Abd al-Rahman I) is one of the most beautiful buildings in the world. The Patio de los Naranjos — 113 orange trees surrounding a Roman fountain — is completely free to enter. The interior (€11) has 856 striped red-and-white columns holding double arches, all lit from above — if the kids have energy for 30 minutes, this is one of the great interiors on earth. The cathedral was inserted into the center of the mosque in the 16th century; both layers are astonishing.
Judería (Jewish Quarter)🚶 3 min from Mezquita — The medieval Jewish quarter directly behind the Mezquita — a maze of whitewashed lanes with iron lanterns and geranium-filled patios. The Calleja de las Flores (2 min from the Mezquita) is the most photographed lane in Córdoba: a narrow whitewashed alley framing the mosque tower at the end. 10 minutes of wandering.
Puente Romano (Roman Bridge)🚶 5 min from Mezquita — A 1st-century BC Roman bridge of 17 arches spanning the Guadalquivir. Walk across for the view back toward the mosque tower and the Calahorra fortress. Flat, stroller-accessible, beautiful.
Alcázar de los Reyes Cristianos🚶 4 min from Mezquita — 14th-century royal palace with extensive terraced gardens — fountains, cypress trees, topiary. The gardens (€4.50) are flat and stroller-friendly. Columbus presented his case to Ferdinand and Isabella here in 1486. Allow 30 minutes.
🍚 Eat in Córdoba
🥚Bar Santos🚶 2 min from MezquitaThe most famous tortilla in Córdoba — enormously thick, made fresh twice daily. €2 a wedge, eaten standing at the bar. The place is unremarkable; the tortilla is not.
🍅SalmorejoCold tomato cream, thicker and richer than gazpacho — a Córdoba invention. €3 at any bar window. Refreshing in the heat.
🍪MantecadosCrumbly almond shortbread — Córdoba's oldest confectionery tradition. Buy a box from any bakery for the drive south.
Timing: Arrive ~12pm, depart by 2pm. 2 hours is plenty for the Mezquita exterior + Judería + Roman Bridge + Bar Santos tortilla. Interior of the Mezquita adds 30–45 minutes.
Granada is the last great Moorish city — the Nasrid dynasty ruled here until 1492, the final year of the Reconquest. The drive from Madrid is 4 hours (vs 2.5 for Córdoba), but the city is 130km closer to Marbella on the other side, so total drive time is comparable. The Alhambra requires advance booking and 3–4 hours; skip the interior today and focus on the Albaicín.
🏛 What to See
Albaicín (Moorish Quarter)🚶 15 min from center — The UNESCO-listed medieval Moorish neighborhood climbing the hillside across from the Alhambra. Narrow cobblestone lanes, whitewashed houses with geraniums, tea houses (teterías), and sudden views of the Alhambra palace complex on the opposite hill. The Albaicín has barely changed in 500 years — this is the living Moorish city the Alhambra's builders lived in.
Mirador de San Nicolás🚶 20 min from Plaza Nueva — The most famous viewpoint in Granada — the Alhambra fills the entire frame, with the snow-capped Sierra Nevada behind it. Gets crowded at sunset; mid-afternoon is better. The cobblestone climb is steep in parts but manageable with a baby carrier.
Plaza Nueva + Cathedral quarter🚶 city center — The main square and the massive Renaissance Cathedral (built from 1523 on the site of the main mosque). The exterior and the adjacent Royal Chapel (€5) where Ferdinand and Isabella are buried are worth 20 minutes. The streets around it have good pastelerías.
🍚 Eat in Granada
🍰Piononos · Casa Isla🚶 Plaza Nueva areaThe iconic Granada pastry: a small cylinder of syrup-soaked sponge rolled around pastry cream and topped with caramelized sugar. Originally from the village of Santa Fe but sold throughout Granada. €1.50 each.
☕Bib-Rambla café terrace🚶 5 min from CathedralThe prettiest square in Granada — a flower market surrounded by café terraces. Coffee under umbrellas with the Cathedral tower above you.
🍰Tortas de aceiteThin, crispy olive oil pastries glazed with anise. Granada bakery staple. Any pastelería.

Old Town walk (before 9am)🚗 15 min — Start at Puerta de las Atarazanas (the old gateway off Calle Ortiz de Molinillo) and walk into the Casco Antiguo. The 16th-century church of Nuestra Señora de la Encarnación was built on the site of the main mosque after the 1487 Reconquest — the clock tower is the original minaret. Plaza de los Naranjos (1485): the two heraldic inscriptions on the Town Hall facade tell the story of the city's reconquest. Walk up Calle Carmen toward the Mirador del Castillo — fortress ruins from the 9th century Moorish citadel with panoramic views over the bay. Best before 9am when the lanes belong to locals and the light is golden.
Puerto Banús🚗 22 min — Completed in 1970 by Jose Banus as a purpose-built marina for the international jet set. The inner port holds the largest yachts — 150–200ft vessels are common, and the scale is genuinely astonishing. Walk the full length of the quay: along the boats outward, then return along the restaurant terrace strip. The morning light reflects off the hulls beautifully. The gate into the inner marina is worth entering — the sheer volume of expensive fiberglass in one place is a spectacle regardless of how you feel about wealth. The designer strip (Versace, Gucci, Banana Republic) behind the quay is for browsing.
Golden Mile + Puente Romano🚗 18 min — The 5km stretch of N-340 between Old Town Marbella and Puerto Banús is called the Milla de Oro. The original luxury hotels here date to the 1950s–1970s — the Marbella Club (1954, founded by Prince Alfonso von Hohenlohe, who essentially invented modern Marbella) and the Puente Romano (built around a genuine 2,000-year-old Roman stone bridge preserved in the hotel grounds) defined the aesthetic of Andalucian luxury that's been copied worldwide. Walk through the Puente Romano gardens to the beach — the hotel generally allows day visitors. The Roman bridge itself is in the hotel grounds behind the main building.
Estepona (optional)🚗 35 min — 20km west of Puerto Banús. Estepona has won the national prize for most flower-decorated town in Spain three consecutive years. The orchid house and tropical botanical garden (free, flat, stroller-perfect) is one of the best free family activities on the Costa. The old town flower murals (painted on whitewashed walls) are genuinely beautiful. Allow 45 minutes.

🗺 Do
Mercado Central (8–9:30am)🚶 6 min · 450m — Built in 1928, one of Europe's largest covered markets (8,000 sq m) under a spectacular Modernista dome of colored glass and ceramic tiles. Over 1,200 stalls — real food, sold fresh, every morning. What makes it different from a tourist market: Valencians still actually shop here daily. The OJ is €1, the jamón stations have entire legs overhead, the bomba rice stalls stock what serious paella cooks use.
Lonja de la Seda (9:30–10:30am)🚶 4 min · 300m — UNESCO World Heritage Site, completed 1498. The Contract Hall (Sala de Contratación) is the finest Late Gothic secular room in Spain: 24 helical columns spiral up 17 metres to a star-ribbed vault flooding with natural light. For 200 years, Valencia's silk trade — the largest in Europe — was conducted under this roof. Free on Sundays (€2 other days). The garden courtyard with orange trees is equally beautiful.
Valencia Cathedral + Holy Grail chapel (optional)🚶 3 min · 200m — Started 1262, built in stages over 300 years. Inside the Capilla del Santo Cáliz: the Santos Cáliz, a 1st-century agate cup that is the Vatican's official Holy Grail candidate. Cathedral museum €9 includes the treasury. The Miguelete tower (207 steps) is skip-worthy with kids.
Torres de Serranos (walk-by)🚶 10 min · 800m — Two Gothic towers built in 1392 as the main ceremonial gate of the old city walls. Free on Sundays. Great for kids to touch 630-year-old stone.
El Carmen neighborhood walk (late morning)🚶 8 min · 600m — The medieval Moorish quarter within the original Roman and Islamic city walls — the oldest inhabited neighborhood in Valencia, and also its most bohemian. The walls themselves are still partially intact (Torres de Serranos marks the north gate, Torres de Quart the west gate). Inside: narrow lanes, baroque churches, entire building facades covered in street murals, independent bookshops, and the best vintage stores in Valencia. Walk Calle de la Bolsería and Calle de Caballeros. The IVAM (Institut Valencià d'Art Modern) sits on the edge of El Carmen — free on Saturdays.
Turia Park + Gulliver (afternoon)🚶 20 min · 1.5km — In 1957 a catastrophic Turia flood killed 81 people. Valencians converted the dried riverbed into a 9km park bisecting the entire city. Rent a 4-seater cargo bike near Gulliver at the east end. Gulliver Park (free): a 70-metre Gulliver pinned to the ground with 30 slides, tunnels, and climbing frames built into his body.

🏰 Hondarribia — Mini-Itinerary
Hondarribia (Fuenterrabía) — 30 min from Arbaso
A medieval walled town on the Bidasoa estuary, directly across the water from France (Hendaye). One of the most beautiful small towns in Spain and a UNESCO candidate.
Casco Medieval (walled old town)🚗 30 min — The walled upper town dates to the 13th century. The walls are largely intact — enter through the Puerta de Santa María. Inside: narrow cobblestone lanes flanked by tall medieval houses with carved wooden balconies and hanging flower baskets, every facade in a different color. The Charles V Castle (now a Parador hotel) dominates the upper town. The Gothic church of Santa María is mid-16th century.
Barrio de la Marina (fishing quarter)🚗 30 min — Below the walled town, the old fishing quarter is completely different in character: brightly painted buildings, hanging geraniums, fishermen's bars along the harbor front. The contrast between the aristocratic upper town and the working-class marina below is part of what makes Hondarribia extraordinary.
Boat crossing to Hendaye, France🚗 30 min — A small passenger boat (txalupa) crosses the Bidasoa river to Hendaye in France every 30 minutes. €1.50 return. Hendaye has a very long beach (4km) and is a pleasant detour. The boat ride itself is charming — kids love it.
Playa de Hondarribia🚗 30 min — A calm, sheltered beach just outside the walled town, facing into the estuary. The water is calmer here than the open Atlantic beaches.
Hermandad de Pescadores🚗 30 min The fishermen's guild restaurant — directly on the harbor. Simple, excellent, anchored in the fishing tradition. Remarkably good for the price.
Pastelería Zubiondo🚗 30 min Good local bakery in the walled town · excellent pastries · stop for coffee before continuing into France
Any marina bar🚗 30 min Any bar along the harbor front in the fishing quarter · vermouth + anchovies · €5 total
🇫🇷 Saint-Jean-de-Luz — Mini-Itinerary
Saint-Jean-de-Luz — 35 min from SS (+ 30 min from Hondarribia)
A Basque fishing town famous for Louis XIV's 1660 wedding here. The beach is sheltered and excellent for young children.
Maison Adam — the original Basque macaron🚗 35 min — Founded 1660 — the original Basque macaron, completely different from French macarons. Almond-based, rustic, chewy. Louis XIV ordered them for his wedding to Marie-Thérèse of Spain (held in this town). The original shop is on Rue Gambetta; buy a box.
Église Saint-Jean-Baptiste🚗 35 min — The church where Louis XIV married in 1660. The interior is the most extraordinary Basque church in France — three tiers of wooden galleries line the walls, a device unique to French Basque churches and designed to separate men (lower galleries) from women (upper galleries). The ornate gilded altarpiece fills the entire east wall. The door Louis XIV used was bricked up after the wedding and has never been reopened.
Place Louis XIV + waterfront🚗 35 min — The main square named for the king, with a bandstand, café terraces, and the harbor behind it. A beautiful 20-minute walk: along the harbor front, through the old town lanes, along the beach promenade.
Plage de Saint-Jean-de-Luz🚗 35 min — The beach is sheltered by two jetties extending into the bay — almost no waves, calm water, excellent for young children. The bay faces north and gets full summer sun from the west in the afternoon. Lifeguarded all summer.
Old Town lanes🚗 35 min — The old town is compact and walkable in 20 minutes. Look for Maison Louis XIV (the house where the king stayed before the wedding — tours available), Rue Gambetta (the main shopping street, Maison Adam, and several good bakeries), and the covered market (Halle de Saint-Jean-de-Luz, open mornings).
🫐Maison Adam macarons🚗 35 minThe original Basque macaron (1660) — almond, chewy, rustic, completely unlike the Pariésian version. Buy a box.
🎂Gâteau BasqueEvery boulangerie in SJdL does a good gâteau Basque — buttery shortcrust with cherry jam or custard.
🥐Pain au chocolatFrench butter transforms this pastry. Any boulangerie in town.
🧀Fromage de brebis (sheep cheese)🚗 35 minThe covered market (mornings) — French Basque sheep cheese, particularly Ossau-Iraty. Eaten with black cherry jam.
🥿EspadrillesSaint-Jean-de-Luz is one of the capitals of espadrille manufacturing. Several shops along Rue Gambetta — classic Basque canvas shoes, good souvenir.

🌊 Biarritz — Mini-Itinerary
Biarritz — 50 min from SS (15 min from SJdL)
Napoleon III's seaside resort, now a surf town with Belle Époque grandeur. La Grande Plage, the Rocher de la Vierge, and Miremont pâtisserie.
La Grande Plage🚗 50 min — The main beach stretches in front of the old casino (now the Biarritz Bellevue conference center). Wide, flat sand, lifeguarded all summer. The surf here is genuine — waves from the Atlantic — so not as calm as SJdL for young children. The Promenade Jacques-Thibaud runs the length of the beach, flat and stroller-accessible.
Rocher de la Vierge🚗 50 min — A volcanic rock arch connected to the mainland by an iron walkway designed by Gustave Eiffel (the same Eiffel). Waves crash through from all sides; on the rock itself, a white Virgin Mary statue watches over the Atlantic. The views from the walkway of the coastline north and south of Biarritz are extraordinary. Access is flat; stroller-accessible on the approach but not on the rock itself in rough weather.
Miremont pâtisserie terrace🚗 50 min — A Belle Époque pâtisserie-salon de thé on the cliff above La Grande Plage since 1872. The glass conservatory terrace — looking down at the beach, the casino, and the open Atlantic — is the best table in Biarritz. Order the mille-feuille. Do not skip this.
Les Halles de Biarritz🚗 50 min — The covered food market (open mornings until about 1pm). Jambon de Bayonne, local cheeses, Piment d'Espelette, Basque farmhouse products. Buy Jambon de Bayonne + baguette + Ossau-Iraty for a beach picnic.
Musée de la Mer (aquarium)🚗 50 min — Art Déco building from 1933 on the seafront rocks. Small but excellent aquarium specializing in the Bay of Biscay — the circular shark pool is the highlight. Good option if it's too hot for the beach. Seal feeding at 10:30am and 5pm.
Plage de la Côte des Basques🚗 50 min — South of the old lighthouse, this beach is sheltered by the cliff and has calmer water than La Grande Plage — better for young children. The cliff-side promenade above it is extraordinarily beautiful.
🍰Miremont mille-feuille🚗 50 minThe glass terrace overlooking La Grande Plage since 1872. The mille-feuille is architectural. The coffee is good. The view is unmissable.
🎂Gâteau Basque🚗 50 minMaison Adam has a branch in Biarritz. Also: Paries (founded 1895) on Place Bellevue has excellent gâteau Basque and its own chocolate line.
🍬Chocolat Pariés🚗 50 minThe most prestigious chocolatier based in the French Basque Country. Founded 1895. Their 'Muxus' (bonbons) and 'Kanouga' (salted butter caramels) are exceptional. Buy the assorted tin.
🥩Jambon de Bayonne + baguette🚗 50 minPaper-thin Jambon de Bayonne from Les Halles, Ossau-Iraty cheese, a baguette. Eat on La Côte des Basques. The best picnic in France.
🌶Espelette pepper chocolate🚗 50 minDark chocolate with Piment d'Espelette (AOC). Buy several bars.

🍫 Bayonne — Optional Add-On
Bayonne (optional) — +30 min from Biarritz
The capital of the French Basque Country. One of Europe's oldest chocolate-making cities — Sephardic Jews expelled from Spain in 1492 brought the process here. Gothic cathedral, half-timbered old town, and three extraordinary chocolate houses.
Grand Bayonne (old town)🚗 1hr 20min — The old town sits at the confluence of the Nive and Adour rivers. Half-timbered buildings in red, green, and blue (the traditional Basque colors) line the riverside quays. The Nive riverfront (Quai Amiral Dubourdieu) is the most beautiful: restaurants and cafés with tables along the water, the old city behind.
Cathédrale Sainte-Marie🚗 1hr 20min — Gothic cathedral begun in 1258, towers completed in the 19th century. The cloister is one of the finest Gothic cloisters in southwest France — stroller-accessible, beautiful proportions. The cathedral interior has medieval stained glass from several centuries.
Chocolate history🚗 1hr 20min — Bayonne became one of the first chocolate-making cities in Europe when Sephardic Jews expelled from Spain and Portugal settled here after 1492, bringing the cacao process from the Americas. By the 17th century, Bayonne chocolate was famous throughout France. Today: Cazenave (since 1854, the oldest), Bonnat (since 1884, the most prestigious single-origin maker in France), and Atelier du Chocolat (the modern brand) are all within 5 minutes of each other.
Les Halles de Bayonne🚗 1hr 20min — The covered market — one of the best in southwest France. Jambon de Bayonne (the AOC ham) is sold here by producers who age it on-site. The market also has excellent foie gras, local cheeses, and Basque products at market prices rather than tourist-shop prices.
☕Cazenave drinking chocolate🚗 1hr 20minThe oldest chocolate house in Bayonne (since 1854). Their specialty is Basque drinking chocolate — made with Valrhona and whisked to a froth in the traditional style. Served in small cups with a pitcher of cream. The salon has barely changed since 1854.
🍫Bonnat single-origin bars🚗 1hr 20minThe most prestigious chocolate maker in Bayonne (since 1884). Their Venezuela and Madagascar single-origin bars are considered among the finest in France. The shop also has gorgeous vintage packaging.
🍬Atelier du Chocolat bonbons🚗 1hr 20minThe modern Bayonnais chocolate brand — excellent bonbons, good seasonal collections.
🥩Jambon de Bayonne at Les Halles🚗 1hr 20minBuy a vacuum-packed whole leg end (€15–20) or just have it sliced paper-thin at a counter stall. The real Jambon de Bayonne — the curing process is controlled by AOC and takes at least 7 months.
🥐Pain au chocolat — any boulangerieIn Bayonne, even the supermarket pain au chocolat is made with proper Basque chocolate. The difference is noticeable.
🌶 Espelette — Optional Inland Stop
Espelette (optional inland) — +30 min inland from Biarritz
A small Basque village famous for the Piment d'Espelette — the mild red pepper (AOC) that flavors most Basque cooking. In June, red peppers hang from every facade to dry.
Village walk🚗 1hr 20min — Espelette is tiny — a 20-minute wander covers the whole thing. Every house has strings of red peppers hanging from the balconies, drying in the summer sun. The village church (built in the classic French Basque style: white walls, dark timber frame, three-tiered wooden galleries inside) and the castle ruins are the main sights. The main street is lined with pepper shops, all selling the same Denominación de Origen product at slightly different prices.
Pepper shopping🚗 1hr 20min — Buy piment d'Espelette ground, whole dried, in olive oil, or as a paste. The spice is mild (not hot), fruity, and aromatic — the pepper that flavors Basque cooking. You can also buy pepper-flavored chocolate, jam, and mustard. Maison Joanto is the most traditional producer.
🌶Piment d'Espelette (ground)🚗 1hr 20minBuy at least 3 jars — ground, paste, and one infused in olive oil. Completely different from any other chili: mild, fruity, aromatic. One of the great flavor discoveries of the trip.
🍫Espelette pepper chocolate🚗 1hr 20minDark chocolate + Piment d'Espelette. The combination is extraordinary — the pepper adds warmth without heat. Buy several bars.

Two tiny Basque fishing towns as the anchors: Getaria for txakoli and Elkano's legendary grilled fish, Zumaia for the flysch cliffs and UNESCO geopark. 🏙 Getaria — Mini-Itinerary
Getaria — 35 min from Arbaso
A medieval fishing village, birthplace of Balenciaga, and home of Elkano — the finest grilled fish restaurant in Spain.
Getaria village + old port🚗 35 min — A medieval fishing village built on a small peninsula — the village plan has not changed in 600 years. The Gothic church of San Salvador (begun 1397) sits at the top, its clock tower visible from the sea. The port below is still working: the fishing boats go out at night and return at dawn, and the catch goes directly to Elkano. Cristóbal Balenciaga (1895–1972) was born in the house on Calle Parrokia — now the Balenciaga Museum.
Balenciaga Museum🚗 35 min — One of the most beautiful fashion museums in the world — designed by Fuensanta Nieto and Enrique Sobejón, embedded into an old palazzo overlooking the sea. The collection traces the couturier's career from his Getaria beginnings to his Paris atelier. Even without a strong interest in fashion, the building and the story of a fisherman's son from this village transforming haute couture are extraordinary. Allow 1–1.5 hours. Stroller-accessible.
Txomin Etxaniz winery🚗 35 min — The most famous txakoli producer in Getaria, with vineyards climbing the hillside above the village and sea views from the winery terrace. Txakoli was nearly extinct 50 years ago — this producer helped lead its revival. The wine is slightly sparkling, bone dry, and poured from height to aerate.
🥂Txakoli glass · any marina bar🚗 35 min€3/glass. Poured from height (30–40cm above the glass) to aerate and create fine bubbles. The local aperitif of Getaria. Drink looking at the fishing harbor and the sea.
🐟Grilled anchovies🚗 35 minBoquerones a la plancha — fresh anchovies grilled whole, squeeze of lemon. Fished from boats visible in the harbor. €4. The freshest anchovies of the trip.
☕Café Kaia Getaria🚗 35 minGood local café near the marina · coffee + toast before Elkano
🧀Idiazabal from Getaria shops🚗 35 minSeveral shops in the old town sell Idiazabal and other Basque cheeses. Buy a wedge to eat with txakoli.
⛏ Zumaia — Mini-Itinerary
Zumaia — 45 min from Arbaso
The flysch cliffs of Itzurun — 60 million years of geological strata exposed by the sea. A UNESCO geopark. Filmed for Game of Thrones (Dragonstone approach).
Itzurun flysch cliffs🚗 45 min — The cliffs at Itzurun beach expose nearly 60 million years of continuous sedimentary strata in near-vertical layers. The K-Pg boundary — the thin layer marking the asteroid impact that killed the dinosaurs 66 million years ago — is visible in the actual rock face. The term 'flysch' for this type of sedimentary rock was named here. The beach path from Zumaia town brings you to the cliff base — flat and stroller-manageable to the beach, then carry for the cliff walk itself.
Flysch coastal walk: Zumaia to Deba🚗 45 min — The 6km coastal walk from Zumaia to Deba follows the cliff tops above the flysch, with views down over the strata from above. One of the great coastal walks in Spain. Takes 1.5–2 hours one way. This is the adult option — the path is not stroller-accessible.
Zumaia town + Plaza Nagusia🚗 45 min — The main square (Plaza Nagusia) has several good bars and is the center of the small town. Worth 20 minutes of wandering before or after the beach. The Zuloaga Museum (in a former convent, with paintings by Ignacio Zuloaga) is sometimes open.
🍦Ice cream · Itzurun beach kiosk🚗 45 minGelato kiosk near the beach path entrance. The reward after the cliff walk.

Deba (optional) — 30 min west of Zumaia

Bílica de la Asunción de Deba🚗 1hr 15min — A 15th-century Gothic basilica in the center of Deba, considered one of the finest Gothic churches in the Basque Country. The portal is extraordinary — polychrome stone carvings of the Last Judgment from around 1500. Free to enter.

Lekeitio (optional — 1 hr from Getaria)
One of the jewels of the Basque coast — a working fishing port with a 15th-century Gothic church, a stunning island beach, and some of the most dramatic scenery on the coast. Worth the drive if you have time.
Isla de San Nicolás🚗 1hr 10min — At low tide, the sea recedes enough to walk across the sandbar to the island of San Nicolás in the middle of the estuary — one of the most extraordinary natural phenomena on the Basque coast. Check tide times before you go. At high tide, the island is surrounded by water and inaccessible.
Playa de Lekeitio🚗 1hr 10min — A beautiful beach sheltered by the island of San Nicolás, with calmer water than the open-Atlantic Basque beaches. The beach is backed by the old town and the Gothic church — one of the most beautiful beach settings in the Basque Country.
Santa María de la Asunción church🚗 1hr 10min — A 15th-century Gothic church that dominates the port. The altarpiece is one of the most elaborate Gothic altarpieces in the Basque Country — carved and polychromed panels telling the story of the Assumption. Free to enter.

Mutriku (optional — 45 min from Getaria)
A small fishing port with medieval tower houses and one of the world's first commercial wave energy plants. An unusual combination of medieval and cutting-edge modern.
Mutriku wave energy plant🚗 50 min — One of the first commercial wave energy plants in the world (opened 2011). The OWC (Oscillating Water Column) system uses the movement of waves to compress air and drive turbines — completely clean energy from the sea. The plant is built into the harbor breakwater and is visible from the harbor. An informational display explains the technology. A genuinely interesting modern infrastructure that most tourists completely miss.
Casco histórico + tower houses🚗 50 min — The old town has several well-preserved medieval tower houses (the defensive towers built by noble families in the 14th–15th centuries). A 15-minute walk through the lanes is enough to see the main sights.

Markina-Xemein (optional inland — 40 min from SS)
An inland Basque town known for traditional pelota and an extraordinary church built around three massive boulders. A complete contrast to the coastal towns.
Ermita de San Miguel de Arretxinaga🚗 40 min — A 15th-century Gothic chapel built around three huge natural boulders — the architect incorporated the rocks directly into the structure, and the interior is partially the rock surface itself. One of the most unusual churches in Spain. Free to enter.
Pelota court + traditional sport🚗 40 min — Markina is considered the birthplace of professional pelota — the Basque ball sport played against a high wall (fronton). The fronton in the town center hosts matches most weekends. Even if no match is scheduled, the fronton itself is architecturally interesting — the proportions and materials are very specifically Basque.
